Harmonizing interoperability specs for AI-driven education & workforce systems.

EDUcore is a shared, AI-ready reference layer that aligns existing education and workforce data standards into a governed semantic backbone. It lets organizations keep the systems they have while exchanging data with clarity, consistency, and trust.

What EDUcore makes possible

Today, sharing learner and workforce data means navigating overlapping specifications, one-off mappings, and brittle integrations. EDUcore changes that by:

  • Aligning existing standards into a common semantic backbone.
  • Publishing governed, machine-readable mappings and crosswalks.
  • Making it easier for AI systems to reason over education data safely.
  • Reducing the cost and risk of interoperability for under-resourced organizations.
Why EDUcore?

Education runs on data. The infrastructure hasn’t kept up.

AI is reshaping how education and workforce systems operate. But interoperability standards and implementation guides remain fragmented across organizations, sectors, and technical stacks. Institutions that need to exchange learner, program, credential, and outcomes data face duplicated integration work, inconsistent meaning, and limited reuse.

Too many specs, not enough alignment

Standards sit in silos, shaped by different communities and contexts. They rarely map cleanly to one another, forcing every new integration to reinvent how fields relate across systems.

Hard for AI to use safely

Existing formats are not designed for modern AI workflows. Meaning is often implicit, mappings are ad hoc, and authoritative definitions are scattered across PDFs and websites instead of machine-readable infrastructure.

Heavy lift for under-resourced orgs

States, districts, colleges, workforce boards, and employers with limited technical capacity pay the highest price. They must choose between bespoke integrations or opting out of data sharing altogether.

EDUcore is not “one more standard.” It is shared infrastructure that makes the standards already in use easier to understand, implement, and connect across the PK20W+ ecosystem.

What We’re Building

An AI-ready interoperability reference layer.

EDUcore establishes a semantic backbone and mapping layer that let organizations keep their existing systems while gaining governed, cross-ecosystem interoperability.

Common semantic backbone

We define a baseline linked-data model in RDF and JSON-LD grounded in the CEDS ontology and informed by credential transparency work, including core entities such as Person, Organization, Credential, Learning Event, Assessment, and Employment Event.

RDF / JSON-LD CEDS ontology Credential alignment

Use-case-driven mappings

Starting from real-world use cases and business drivers, we map high-value standards, profiles, and data elements into the baseline model. Crosswalks become explicit, versioned, and auditable instead of improvised in one-off spreadsheets.

Crosswalks Versioned mappings Business-aligned

Reference library for implementers

We publish a searchable reference library that includes canonical definitions, mappings, implementation notes, openness status, and governance metadata. Implementers get a practical guide, not just a conceptual model.

Developer-friendly Implementation notes Governance metadata

Future-proofed, safe-by-design architecture

Working with technical leaders and platform providers, we ensure the architecture supports modern AI workflows while embedding guardrails for privacy, provenance, auditability, and human oversight in high-stakes contexts.

Privacy & provenance Auditability Human-in-the-loop
Near-Term Deliverables

Version 0.1: what’s coming first.

The first phase creates the planning and design foundation for subsequent build work. It focuses on connecting stakeholders, clarifying use cases, and demonstrating the value of an RDF-based interoperability layer for AI.

Stakeholder registry & resource catalog

A comprehensive registry of stakeholders and catalog of relevant resources spanning PK20W+ and workforce ecosystems. This includes an expanded community of practice where providers and experts can connect more effectively.

  • Networked communities of practice.
  • Discovery of standards, profiles, and tools.
  • On-ramps for new participants, including AI labs and platform partners.

Use case taxonomy mapped to P20W+ ontological model

A taxonomy of use cases organized by business driver and mapped to the specific data elements needed, expressed in an ontological model built with RDF and aligned to the open CEDS community.

  • Business-driven use case catalog.
  • Data elements and option sets mapped to an ontological model.
  • Support for longitudinal, cross-system workflows.

Demonstration applications

Demonstration of how the RDF-based model can support concrete use cases, including Model Context Protocol (MCP) patterns and other AI-driven workflows that rely on governed, interoperable data.

  • Working examples of RDF in action.
  • AI-ready data structures for safe integration.
  • Foundations for reusable reference implementations.
Three-Year Vision

From project to shared public infrastructure.

Within three years, EDUcore aims to operate as trusted infrastructure for the education and workforce ecosystem — a semantic backbone that reduces risk, cost, and fragmentation while enabling AI to work with governed data structures.

  • Recognized reference layer: EDUcore serves as the interoperability layer for PK20W+ education and workforce systems, with active participation from standards bodies, platform providers, states, employers, credentialing organizations, and AI labs.
  • Production-grade ontological model: A publicly maintained ontological model in RDF and JSON-LD, grounded in CEDS and aligned with related specifications, backed by versioned, auditable mappings across major standards.
  • Reusable reference implementations: Initial demos mature into reference implementations that show how AI systems can safely support crosswalk generation, validation, and compliance by relying on governed semantics.
  • Lower cost and risk for all: States, districts, colleges, workforce boards, and employers — including under-resourced organizations — can participate in secure, standards-aligned data exchange without bespoke plumbing for every connection.
